# Energy Context of Rio Grande do Sul (RS) and Porto Alegre (POA)

## 1. Current Energy Context of Rio Grande do Sul

### 1.1 Energy supply and generation

* **Installed capacity & growth** – According to the state’s socio‑economic atlas, the installed electricity generating capacity in RS increased from **5.24 GW in 2010 to 8.66 GW in 2022**, reflecting a strong expansion in renewable generation【687507562469781†screenshot】.
* **Composition of the electricity mix (2022)** – Data from the same atlas show that the state’s electric‑energy matrix is dominated by **hydroelectricity (58.6 %)**, followed by **thermoelectric generation (18 %)**, mostly coal and natural gas, **wind power (17.6 %)** and **solar power (5.7 %)**【98992319010935†screenshot】.  The diversification has been driven by growth of wind and solar projects and increased use of natural gas and biomass【98992319010935†screenshot】.
* **National context** – Rio Grande do Sul operates within Brazil’s national interconnected system (SIN), and in 2022 its installed capacity represented about **4.6 % of Brazil’s total**【687507562469781†screenshot】.  Because of low hydro inflows in recent years the state has sometimes relied on imports from other regions【88308031272635†L875-L881】.
* **Renewability** – The state maintains one of the cleanest energy matrices in Brazil; in 2022 **87.5 % of electricity generated was from renewable sources** (hydro, wind, solar, biomass)【88308031272635†L728-L873】.  The high share of renewables is reinforced by a combination of hydropower dams, two large coal plants (Candiota III and Pampa Sul) and rapidly expanding wind and solar farms【88308031272635†L331-L359】.
* **Emerging renewables** – The InvestRS agency notes that RS is Brazil’s **4th largest solar producer and 5th largest wind producer**.  Estimates suggest the state has **103 GW of onshore wind potential**, **114 GW of offshore wind potential**, and **1 700 TWh yr⁻¹ of photovoltaic potential**, highlighting an enormous resource base【478543290755187†L31-L60】.  RS also holds **28.5 billion t of coal reserves (89 % of Brazil’s total)**【478543290755187†L31-L60】, explaining why coal remains part of the mix.

### 1.2 Energy demand

* **Sectoral consumption** – Transport is the largest energy‑consuming sector in RS, accounting for **about 45 % of final energy use** in 2022, followed by industry, residential and agriculture【88308031272635†L909-L1007】.  Transport consumption still relies heavily on diesel and gasoline; renewable share in transport comes mainly from ethanol (64.7 %) and biodiesel blends (32.6 %)【88308031272635†L909-L1007】.  Industry uses mainly electricity, wood and biomass, giving it a high renewable share【88308031272635†L1039-L1083】.
* **Transmission network** – The state’s transmission network has about **66 substations** and is linked to Argentina and Uruguay through converter stations; lines operate at 230, 138 and 69 kV【721194622874583†screenshot】.  Since 2021 the network has been operated by **CPFL Energia** following the privatization of the state utility CEEE‑T【721194622874583†screenshot】.

### 1.3 Role of Porto Alegre

* **Consumption center** – Porto Alegre, the state capital, is one of RS’s largest energy demand centers but produces little electricity.  The city depends on the national grid and on state‑level generation (hydro, wind, coal).  Recent storms and floods have exposed vulnerabilities in its distribution network.
* **Electricity procurement and energy efficiency** – The municipality has been purchasing **100 % renewable energy** for about **60 public facilities** (hospitals, schools and administration buildings) and has initiated energy‑efficiency projects through LED lighting and building retrofits【466834624064515†L90-L122】.  It is also introducing **100 electric buses** and building charging infrastructure【612339836068199†L81-L108】.

## 2. Main Energy Problems Over the Last Years

### 2.1 Dependence on coal and fossil fuels

* **Coal plants** – RS hosts Brazil’s two remaining coal‑fired power plants (Candiota III – 350 MW and Pampa Sul – 345 MW), which provide dispatchable power but contribute to greenhouse‑gas emissions and local pollution【88308031272635†L331-L359】.  Coal also fuels cement and petrochemical industries in the Baixo Jacuí and Campanha regions【779956351242911†screenshot】.
* **Transport emissions** – The transport sector remains heavily dependent on diesel and gasoline, with only a small proportion of electric or low‑carbon vehicles【88308031272635†L909-L1007】.

### 2.2 Aging distribution network and extreme‑weather vulnerability

* **Storm‑related outages** – In January 2024 a violent storm with wind gusts over 100 km h⁻¹ knocked down trees and power lines, leaving **over 1.1 million consumers in RS without electricity**.  Three days after the storm, about **600 000 customers** were still without power【937458241423748†screenshot】.  Residents of Porto Alegre blocked streets and built barricades to protest the slow restoration by private utilities (CEEE Equatorial and RGE)【937458241423748†screenshot】.  Former employees noted that when the utility was state‑owned, maintenance crews responded more quickly【308812370563199†screenshot】.
* **May 2024 floods** – Historic flooding in May 2024 displaced over 600 000 people; **138 000 people in Porto Alegre lost electricity and drainage pumps shut down** because the power system collapsed【305645961824061†L264-L269】.  The blackout demonstrated the vulnerability of the grid to climate‑driven events and the need for resilient infrastructure.
* **Low quality of service** – After privatization of CEEE, customers have reported longer outages and slower repairs【308812370563199†screenshot】.  Aging distribution networks, lack of tree‑trimming, and under‑investment have reduced service quality.

### 2.3 Hydro dependency and droughts

* **Hydro variability** – Hydropower provides most of the state’s electricity; when water levels are low, RS imports power from other regions or increases coal generation.  During the 2015‑2022 period hydropower output declined, prompting more thermal generation【88308031272635†L788-L795】.

### 2.4 Economic and social issues

* **Just transition challenges** – Coal‑mining regions such as Baixo Jacuí and Campanha are economically dependent on coal.  Without a fair transition, the closure of mines and plants could cause unemployment and social disruption【779956351242911†screenshot】.

## 3. Existing Plans and Policies

### 3.1 State‑level plans

1. **Plano Estadual de Transição Energética Justa (State Plan for a Just Energy Transition, TEJ)** – Launched in 2024 under the programme *Avançar na Sustentabilidade*, this plan seeks to phase out coal while **promoting economic diversification and social inclusion** in coal‑dependent regions【353939390523916†L249-L360】.  The plan emphasizes:
   * gradual and participatory migration from fossil fuels to renewable energy;
   * support for workers and communities through training, green jobs and social protection;
   * development of renewable projects (solar, wind, biomass, green hydrogen) in Baixo Jacuí and Campanha【779956351242911†screenshot】;
   * partnerships and green finance to attract investment【353939390523916†L249-L360】.
2. **Book of Opportunities & InvestRS initiatives** – At COP30 in 2025, RS presented a *Book of Opportunities* comprising **44 projects** with **4.3 GW of renewable capacity under licensing** (wind, hydro, biogas) and aims to attract **R$ 57.6 billion** of investments【932868930323711†L66-L122】.  InvestRS emphasises the state’s **85 % renewable electricity matrix** and its ranking among the top Brazilian producers of solar, wind and biofuels【932868930323711†L66-L122】.  The agency promotes green hydrogen hubs, biogas projects and electric‑mobility initiatives.
3. **Hydrogen & offshore wind collaboration** – RS signed memorandums of understanding with the **Port of Rotterdam** and **Neoenergia** to develop **green‑hydrogen hubs** and **onshore/offshore wind** projects【93711727352514†L954-L968】.  A 2023 chapter notes that these agreements aim to leverage the state’s renewable resources and port infrastructure for exporting green fuels.
4. **Green Ports Program / Energy Port Platform** – Portos RS created the **Energy Port Platform** to turn the Port of Rio Grande into a renewable‑energy hub.  A presentation lists several advantages: licensed industrial district with available land, robust port infrastructure, proximity to the coast (reducing logistics costs), skilled labour and maritime industry clusters【735620642379617†L24-L49】.  It highlights the **Rio Grande refinery**, which will become **Brazil’s first bio‑refinery** with a **green‑hydrogen project in progress**【735620642379617†L83-L86】.  The platform aims to decarbonize logistics and attract offshore wind and green‑ammonia investments.
5. **Clean‑energy procurement and public buildings** – The state government purchases renewable electricity for its facilities and encourages energy efficiency in public buildings.  A 2023 state news release indicated efforts to continue the TEJ plan (although the page later returned 404, the announcement is referenced in the investRS documents).

### 3.2 Major project proposals

1. **Aura Sul floating offshore wind pilot** – A consortium of multinational companies, the Federal University of Rio Grande do Sul, Portos RS and Sindienergia is developing **Brazil’s first floating offshore wind power plant (Aura Sul Wind)** near the Port of Rio Grande.  The pilot (announced in June 2025) will use Japanese **Raijin Floating Offshore Wind Turbine (FOWT)** technology.  A 200‑m‑tall tower with 150‑m blades will generate **18 MW**, and the structure will be assembled in the port complex before being towed offshore【151850302025311†screenshot】.  Operations are scheduled to start in 2030.  The project is part of the **Green Ports Program** and is expected to cost **about US$ 100 million**【717591098422692†screenshot】.  Stakeholders emphasise its role in creating a **local supply chain**, stimulating shipbuilding and offshore‑energy services and positioning Rio Grande as a hub for floating wind【717591098422692†screenshot】.
2. **Large‑scale offshore wind farms** – EDF Renewables and other developers have applied for environmental licences for several offshore wind projects in RS.  One EnergyCentral report noted three EDF projects totalling **7.28 GW** (1 596 MW, 1 659 MW and 3 024 MW) offshore RS【776136902569731†L56-L73】.  Brazilian authorities are also preparing a national framework for offshore wind; RS is expected to host many of the early projects due to its high wind potential.
3. **Onshore wind and solar expansions** – Numerous onshore wind and solar farms are under development.  The state aims to add more than 4 GW of renewable capacity through projects listed in the Book of Opportunities【932868930323711†L66-L122】.  RS already hosts one of Brazil’s largest wind clusters in the northern coast and Pampa region.
4. **Green‑hydrogen and ammonia plants** – Local governments and private companies are planning **green‑hydrogen plants** linked to the Port of Rio Grande.  The city of Rio Grande announced a project to build the **first green hydrogen and ammonia plant in the state** (approximate investment not publicly disclosed).  The state also signed a letter of intent with 24 Dutch companies to collaborate on green‑hydrogen development【735620642379617†L101-L107】.
5. **Bio‑metane projects** – Porto Alegre joined the **Pacto do Biometano** (biomethane pact) to convert solid waste from regional landfills into biomethane.  Landfills in Minas do Leão and São Leopoldo already produce about **100 000 m³ day⁻¹** of biomethane【307713152290102†L81-L124】.

## 4. Role of Porto Alegre and City‑Level Plans

### 4.1 Porto Alegre Climate Action Plan (PLAC)

Porto Alegre has been developing a **Plano de Ação Climática (PLAC)** that was almost complete before the May 2024 flood.  The plan identifies emission sources and climate risks and proposes mitigation and adaptation actions:

* **Risk mapping** – The plan maps six key threats: **fluvial floods, landslides/erosion, heat waves, droughts, vector‑borne diseases and storms**, using scenarios for 2030 and 2050【182579112197824†screenshot】.  Preliminary risk maps correctly predicted that areas near BR‑290/BR‑116, the airport and access to the Guaíba bridge would be flooded during the May 2024 storm【189561674641783†screenshot】【750386411772273†screenshot】.
* **Mitigation pillars** – The plan is structured around three pillars: **POA Baixo Carbono** (low‑carbon city) aiming to reduce emissions from transport, waste and energy; **POA Resiliente** focusing on climate‑risk preparedness; and **POA Verde e Azul** targeting ecosystem restoration and green infrastructure【466834624064515†L90-L122】.
* **Specific actions** – Actions include:
  * **Electrification of public transport** – purchase of **100 electric buses** and installation of charging infrastructure【612339836068199†L81-L108】;
  * **Clean‑energy procurement** – contracting renewable electricity for **60 public facilities**【466834624064515†L90-L122】;
  * **Urban terrariums and green infrastructure** – constructing terrariums (infiltration gardens), planting **20 000+ trees**, creating biodiversity corridors and maintaining an inventory of urban trees to reduce heat‑island effects【612339836068199†L81-L108】【307713152290102†L81-L124】;
  * **Biomethane and waste‑to‑fuel initiatives** – participating in the *Pacto de Biometano* to convert municipal solid waste into biomethane for buses and municipal vehicles【307713152290102†L81-L124】;
  * **Climate monitoring and alerts** – expanding monitoring systems and emergency alerts to improve preparedness【466834624064515†L90-L122】;
  * **Drainage and flood protection works** – implementing major drainage works, rehabilitating pump stations, dikes and floodgates using funding of about **R$ 6 billion**【466834624064515†L90-L122】.

### 4.2 City’s role in state energy transition

* **Model for urban decarbonisation** – As the largest urban center in RS, Porto Alegre’s actions on electrified transport, biomethane use, renewable procurement and climate adaptation serve as a **demonstration for other cities**.  The PLAC and the city’s participation in global initiatives (e.g., Beat the Heat, Pacto de Biometano) highlight a proactive approach to sustainable urban energy【307713152290102†L81-L124】.
* **Demand management** – By reducing consumption through efficiency and electrifying transport, the city eases pressure on the state grid and contributes to the overall decarbonisation goals.
* **Resilience** – The May 2024 floods illustrated that climate risk is a major energy‑system issue.  Porto Alegre’s plan to enhance drainage, strengthen pump stations, and integrate climate risk into urban planning is essential for long‑term energy security.【466834624064515†L90-L122】.

## 5. Recommendations for Long‑Term Stability and Decarbonisation

1. **Accelerate the Just Energy Transition** – Prioritize retirement of coal plants while ensuring economic diversification and social protection in coal‑dependent regions.  Invest in retraining programmes and create clean‑energy manufacturing (wind turbine blades, solar panels, green‑hydrogen equipment) to absorb displaced workers.
2. **Strengthen grid resilience** – Modernize distribution networks, increase tree‑trimming and maintenance, and implement micro‑grid and battery‑storage systems to prevent outages during storms.  Consider burying lines in critical urban areas and hardening substations against floods.  
3. **Expand renewable generation** – Fast‑track environmental licensing for wind and solar projects, including the **Aura Sul** pilot and larger offshore wind farms.  Promote rooftop solar and community solar projects in cities.  Use the high biomass availability (rice husk, black liquor, forest residues) for dispatchable bio‑energy【721194622874583†screenshot】.
4. **Develop green‑hydrogen and biometane hubs** – Utilize the port infrastructure to produce green hydrogen and ammonia for export and local industry.  Support pilot plants and create incentives for domestic consumption (e.g., fertiliser production, heavy transport).  Expand biomethane projects to use landfill gas and agricultural waste【307713152290102†L81-L124】.
5. **Enhance electric mobility** – Scale up electrification of buses and taxis, implement EV charging networks, and support adoption of e‑bikes and last‑mile electric logistics.  Combine with renewable electricity procurement to maximise emissions reduction. 
6. **Implement integrated climate‑energy planning** – Align state and municipal climate plans with energy strategies.  Use updated flood and heat‑wave projections to guide location of critical infrastructure.  Encourage citizen participation and transparency to maintain political support.

## 6. Conclusion

Rio Grande do Sul has a **highly renewable electricity matrix** but faces significant challenges—dependence on coal for firm capacity, vulnerability of distribution networks to extreme weather, and the socio‑economic transition of coal regions.  The **Just Energy Transition Plan** and initiatives to develop **offshore wind**, **green hydrogen**, **biomethane** and **electric mobility** show that the state is positioning itself as a national leader in clean energy.  Porto Alegre’s climate action plan complements this by focusing on urban decarbonisation, adaptation and renewable procurement.  Successful implementation of these plans will require coordinated investment, robust regulation and inclusive policies that ensure workers and communities benefit from the energy transition.
