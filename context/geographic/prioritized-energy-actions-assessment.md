## 1. Executive Summary

Porto Alegre sits at the intersection of three reinforcing policy layers — Brazil's national energy transition framework, Rio Grande do Sul's Just Energy Transition Plan (TEJ) and ProClima 2050 strategy, and the city's own Climate Action Plan (PLAC). This nested alignment creates an unusually strong enabling environment for energy actions, particularly those addressing grid resilience, distributed solar, building efficiency, and transport electrification.

The May 2024 floods — which left 138,000 people without power and disabled critical drainage pump stations — fundamentally reshaped priorities. Grid resilience and community-scale energy security have moved from "nice to have" to existential necessities. At the same time, Porto Alegre's existing commitments (100 electric buses, renewable procurement for 60 public facilities, Pacto do Biometano) provide implementation momentum that newer actions can leverage.

This assessment identifies **4 priority actions**, **3 complementary actions**, and **3 enabling/conditional actions** that form a coherent portfolio. The priority actions address the most urgent needs (grid resilience, distributed generation) while aligning with all three policy levels and available financing. The portfolio is designed to maximize synergies — solar on public buildings pairs with efficiency retrofits; biomethane connects to bus fleet electrification; community solar+storage directly addresses flood resilience.

The scoring framework weights context alignment (25%), commitment alignment (20%), impact potential (20%), financing readiness (15%), implementation readiness (10%), and portfolio synergy (10%). Actions are classified as Priority (≥70), Complementary (55–69), or Enabling/Conditional (<55) based on composite scores.

## 2. Nested Policy Alignment Matrix

### 2.1 Brazil National Level

- **Brazil MMGD (Micro and Mini Distributed Generation)**: Net metering/net billing framework enables rooftop and community solar. Under review but currently active.
- **Free market opening**: High-voltage consumers already eligible; low-voltage commercial/industrial from Aug 2026; residential from Dec 2027. This progressively enables new business models for distributed energy.
- **National Interconnected System (SIN)**: RS contributes ~4.6% of Brazil's installed capacity. Grid interconnection provides backup but also exposes POA to national supply disruptions.
- **National renewable targets**: Brazil's energy plan emphasizes diversification beyond hydro toward wind, solar, biomass, and green hydrogen.
- **Climate commitments**: Brazil's NDC and participation in global frameworks create pressure for subnational action.

### 2.2 Rio Grande do Sul State Level

- **Just Energy Transition Plan (TEJ, 2024)**: Phases out coal while promoting economic diversification. Directly relevant to RS's two remaining coal plants (Candiota III 350 MW, Pampa Sul 345 MW).
- **ProClima 2050**: 50% GHG reduction by 2030, carbon neutrality by 2050. Provides overarching state framework.
- **Book of Opportunities**: 44 projects, 4.3 GW renewable capacity under licensing, R$ 57.6 billion target investment.
- **Regulatory package**: Lei 14.014/2012 (wind), Lei 14.898/2016 (solar incentives), Lei 14.864/2016 (biomethane/biogas), LC 872/2020 (sustainability policy). These state laws directly enable municipal programs.
- **Green Hydrogen & Offshore Wind**: MoUs with Port of Rotterdam and Neoenergia; Aura Sul floating offshore wind pilot (18 MW, ~US$100M, operations 2030); EDF projects totaling 7.28 GW.
- **RS energy matrix**: 87.5% renewable (hydro 58.6%, wind 17.6%, solar 5.7%, thermal 18%). Enormous untapped potential: 103 GW onshore wind, 114 GW offshore wind, 1,700 TWh/yr PV.

### 2.3 Porto Alegre City Level

- **PLAC (Climate Action Plan)**: Three pillars — POA Baixo Carbono (low carbon), POA Resiliente, POA Verde e Azul (green/blue infrastructure).
- **Active commitments**: 100 electric buses + charging infrastructure; renewable procurement for 60 public facilities; 10 biodigesters in schools; LED lighting programs; Pacto do Biometano; R$ 6 billion drainage/flood protection.
- **Energy actions in PLAC**: POA-E-06 (sustainable buildings & green tech), POA-E-07 (energy transition in public buildings), POA-E-08 (sustainable logistics plan).
- **Post-flood reality**: May 2024 floods displaced 600,000+ people statewide; 138,000 in POA lost power; pump stations failed. This elevates resilience from a secondary consideration to a primary driver.
- **Targets**: 100% public buildings with renewable energy by 2030; 8% residential / 10% commercial buildings with distributed renewables by 2030, scaling to 40%/50% by 2050.

## 4. Prioritized Energy Actions

### 4.1 Tier 1 — Priority Actions (Score ≥ 70)

#### 4.1.1 Distribution Grid Resilience Upgrade

- **Catalog ID**: `grid-resilience-upgrade`
- **Estimated Score**: **~82** (highest context alignment)
- **Why Priority**: The single most urgent action. Jan 2024 storm left 1.1M without power in RS; May 2024 floods collapsed POA's grid, disabling pump stations and leaving 138K without electricity. Post-privatization service quality has declined. Every other energy action depends on a functioning grid.
- **Policy alignment**: Triple-aligned (SIN reliability, TEJ, PLAC Resiliente)
- **Key activities**: Substation flood-proofing, undergrounding critical lines, smart switches, vegetation management, micro-grid islands for critical facilities (hospitals, pump stations)
- **Scale**: 5–50 km of network per phase
- **Financing**: DFI concessional loans (high interest post-disaster), green bonds. Funder priority: resilience is top category.
- **Implementation timeline**: 18–36 months
- **Critical dependency**: Requires utility cooperation (CEEE Equatorial / RGE). Post-privatization governance is a risk.
- **PLAC link**: Actions 21–22 (flood/drainage infrastructure depend on reliable power for pump stations)

#### 4.1.2 Solar PV on Public Buildings

- **Catalog ID**: `solar-public-buildings`
- **Estimated Score**: **~78**
- **Why Priority**: POA already procures renewable energy for 60 public facilities — rooftop solar is the natural next step. PLAC target: 100% public buildings with renewables by 2030. State solar incentive law (Lei 14.898) active. PPP model already in use for PV installation in municipal buildings.
- **Policy alignment**: Triple-aligned (MMGD, Lei 14.898, POA-E-07)
- **Key activities**: Structural assessments, rooftop PV installation via PPP, net metering enrollment, monitoring
- **Scale**: 0.05–5 MWp per batch; city has hundreds of eligible buildings including schools
- **Financing**: Municipal budget (quick payback), climate fund grants, PPP/ESCO model
- **Implementation timeline**: 6–18 months
- **Co-benefits**: Budget savings (15–30% electricity bill reduction), demonstration effect, pairs with green roofs (NbS)
- **PLAC link**: POA-E-07 sub-actions 7.1, 7.3; target 40% energy-self-sufficient schools by 2030

#### 4.1.3 Community Solar + Storage for Resilience

- **Catalog ID**: `community-solar-resilience`
- **Estimated Score**: **~74**
- **Why Priority**: Directly addresses the May 2024 flood lesson — when the grid fails, vulnerable communities lose everything. Community solar+storage provides 4–12 hours backup for critical loads. Addresses both energy poverty and resilience. Post-disaster context unlocks recovery financing.
- **Policy alignment**: Triple-aligned (MMGD shared generation, ProClima resilience, PLAC Resiliente)
- **Key activities**: Site identification in flood-affected neighborhoods, community engagement, shared generation regulatory setup, PV + battery installation
- **Scale**: 0.1–2 MWp + MWh storage per site
- **Financing**: Climate fund grants (strong fit), PPP-light, DFI post-disaster windows
- **Implementation timeline**: 12–24 months
- **Equity dimension**: Targets 50–500 low-income households per installation; social tariff beneficiaries
- **Key risk**: "Emerging" maturity tier — fewer precedents in Brazil. Community governance model needed.
- **Geospatial priority**: Use flood depth maps (Copernicus EMSN194) + population vulnerability (IBGE) to site installations

#### 4.1.4 Energy Efficiency Retrofit — Public Buildings

- **Catalog ID**: `building-efficiency-public`
- **Estimated Score**: **~72**
- **Why Priority**: Lowest-hanging fruit with shortest payback. POA has started LED and retrofit projects but hasn't completed city-wide rollout. Pairs naturally with solar PV — efficiency first, then generation. PLS (Decree 21.869/2023) already mandates behavioral change across 20,000+ employees.
- **Policy alignment**: Triple-aligned (national efficiency standards, ProClima, POA-E-06/E-08)
- **Key activities**: Energy audits, LED lighting, HVAC optimization, insulation, smart building controls
- **Scale**: 5–100 buildings per phase
- **Financing**: Municipal budget (short payback), climate fund grants
- **Implementation timeline**: 6–12 months (fastest of all actions)
- **Co-benefits**: 20–40% cost savings, improved occupant comfort, reduced grid load
- **Sequencing**: Should precede or run in parallel with solar PV — reduce demand first, then right-size generation

### 4.2 Tier 2 — Complementary Actions (Score 55–69)

#### 4.2.1 Electric Bus Fleet Transition

- **Catalog ID**: `electric-bus-fleet`
- **Estimated Score**: **~67**
- **Why Complementary (not Priority)**: Strong commitment alignment (100 e-buses in PLAC) but complex prerequisites — route electrification studies, grid capacity at depots, concession contract negotiations. Transport is RS's largest energy sector (~45% of final energy), so impact is significant but timeline is longer.
- **Policy alignment**: Triple-aligned (national e-mobility, TEJ transport decarbonization, PLAC commitment)
- **Key activities**: Route feasibility studies, charging infrastructure deployment at depots, bus procurement, grid capacity upgrades at depot sites
- **Scale**: 10–500 buses; POA has committed to 100 as first tranche
- **Financing**: DFI concessional, climate fund grants, green bonds — funder priority category
- **Implementation timeline**: 12–36 months
- **Impact**: 40–80 tCO2e/bus/year; significant PM2.5 and NOx reduction along routes
- **Synergy**: Pairs with biomethane (transitional fuel for non-electrified routes) and grid resilience (depot charging needs reliable power)

#### 4.2.2 Biomethane from Municipal Solid Waste

- **Catalog ID**: `biomethane-waste`
- **Estimated Score**: **~65**
- **Why Complementary**: Active commitment (Pacto do Biometano, 10 school biodigesters) and existing production (Minas do Leão and São Leopoldo landfills produce ~100,000 m³/day). State-level RS Biogas Program (RS-E-02) provides regulatory and financial framework. But requires environmental licensing and offtake agreements.
- **Policy alignment**: Triple-aligned (national biogas framework, RS-E-02, Pacto do Biometano)
- **Key activities**: Landfill gas capture optimization, new anaerobic digestion facilities, biomethane purification for vehicle fuel or grid injection, offtake agreements with bus fleet
- **Scale**: 10,000–200,000 m³/day
- **Financing**: PPP-light, DFI concessional, climate fund grants
- **Implementation timeline**: 18–36 months
- **Co-benefits**: Avoided methane emissions (GWP 28x), reduced landfill volume, biofertilizer production
- **Synergy**: Natural pairing with e-bus transition (biomethane as transitional fuel) and school biodigesters (POA-E-07)

#### 4.2.3 LED Street Lighting Conversion

- **Catalog ID**: `street-lighting-led`
- **Estimated Score**: **~62**
- **Why Complementary**: Quick win with strong financial payback (40–65% cost savings). POA has started LED initiatives but full city-wide conversion is incomplete. ESCO/PPP-light model is well suited. Less transformative than other actions but highly bankable.
- **Policy alignment**: Double-aligned (ProClima efficiency, PLAC LED initiatives)
- **Key activities**: Street lighting inventory, procurement (PPP or direct), phased installation, smart dimming controls
- **Scale**: 5,000–200,000 luminaires
- **Financing**: Municipal budget, PPP-light (ESCO model), DFI concessional
- **Implementation timeline**: 6–18 months
- **Impact**: 50–70% energy reduction per luminaire; public safety co-benefits
- **Sequencing**: Can proceed independently and quickly — good "early win" for portfolio credibility

### 4.3 Tier 3 — Enabling / Conditional Actions

#### 4.3.1 Urban Tree Canopy for Cooling and Grid Resilience

- **Source**: POA-RES-E-01, POA Verde e Azul (Action 27)
- **Estimated Score**: ~50
- **Why Enabling**: Not a direct energy generation or efficiency action, but provides critical co-benefits — 5–10°C cooling in urban canyons reduces cooling energy demand and transformer overload risk during heat waves. Protects distribution infrastructure from heat stress. POA has committed to 20,000+ trees.
- **Spatial targeting**: Use NDVI maps to identify low-canopy urban heat island zones; overlay with energy infrastructure corridors
- **Implementation**: Neighborhood-scale; relies on municipal green infrastructure programs
- **Relevance to action selection**: Should be scored as a resilience multiplier for grid upgrade and building efficiency actions, not as a standalone energy action

#### 4.3.2 Green Hydrogen Program (state-level, city as demand node)

- **Source**: RS-E-01
- **Estimated Score**: ~45
- **Why Conditional**: Green hydrogen is a state-level initiative centered on the Port of Rio Grande. POA's role is as a consumption and distribution node, particularly for the 4th District industrial redevelopment. Timeline is long (2030+) and investment is enormous. Important for strategic planning but not actionable at city pre-feasibility scale.
- **POA relevance**: Industrial zones, heavy transport corridors. Watch for RS-level pilot projects that could create local demand signals.
- **Relevance to action selection**: Track as a future context signal, not a near-term action

#### 4.3.3 Carbon Market and Industrial Decarbonization

- **Source**: RS-E-04
- **Estimated Score**: ~42
- **Why Conditional**: RS is developing carbon market strategies; POA's industrial sector (especially 4th District) will face decarbonization pressures. Carbon credit revenues could fund NbS and energy projects. But the regulatory framework is still emerging and the market is not yet operational.
- **POA relevance**: Industrial zone decarbonization; potential revenue source for urban reforestation (NbS carbon credits)
- **Relevance to action selection**: Include as a financing signal rather than a standalone action. Carbon credit potential can boost the financial case for other actions (solar, biomethane, urban forests)

## 5. Action Synergies and Portfolio Logic

### 5.1 Synergy Clusters

**Cluster A — Public Building Energy Transformation**
- Efficiency Retrofit → Solar PV → Green Roof (NbS) → Biodigesters (schools)
- Logic: Reduce demand first, then right-size generation, then add NbS co-benefits. School biodigesters add waste-to-energy loop.
- Shared financing: Climate fund grant or PPP can cover the bundle.
- PLAC actions: POA-E-06, POA-E-07, POA-E-08

**Cluster B — Grid Resilience + Community Energy Security**
- Grid Resilience Upgrade → Community Solar+Storage → Urban Tree Canopy
- Logic: Harden the grid backbone, then add distributed resilience for vulnerable neighborhoods, then reduce thermal stress on infrastructure.
- Post-flood financing: DFI recovery windows, climate adaptation funds.
- PLAC actions: POA-RES-E-01, Actions 21–22

**Cluster C — Transport + Waste Circular Economy**
- Electric Bus Fleet → Biomethane from MSW → Biogas in Schools
- Logic: Biomethane as transitional fuel for routes not yet electrified; school biodigesters build supply chain knowledge; e-buses are the end state.
- Shared infrastructure: Bus depots, charging/fueling stations, waste collection logistics.
- PLAC actions: PLAC transport commitment, Pacto do Biometano, POA-E-07

**Cluster D — Quick Wins for Credibility**
- LED Street Lighting → Building Efficiency Retrofit
- Logic: Fast-implementing, high-visibility, strong payback. Builds political capital and institutional capacity for larger actions.
- Timeline: 6–18 months for both.

### 5.2 Sequencing Recommendations

**Phase 1 (0–12 months) — Foundation and Quick Wins**
1. Energy efficiency retrofit of public buildings (6–12 months, lowest complexity)
2. LED street lighting conversion (6–18 months, strong payback)
3. Grid resilience assessment and initial hardening (starts immediately, 18+ months total)
4. Community solar+storage site identification and community engagement

**Phase 2 (6–24 months) — Core Deployment**
5. Solar PV on public buildings (6–18 months, follows efficiency audits)
6. Community solar+storage first installations in flood-affected areas
7. Electric bus fleet — first 100 buses + charging infrastructure
8. Biomethane offtake agreements and plant optimization

**Phase 3 (18–36 months) — Scale and Integration**
9. Grid resilience full deployment (undergrounding, smart switches)
10. Solar PV scale-up to remaining public buildings
11. E-bus fleet expansion beyond 100
12. Biomethane grid injection or expanded vehicle fuel use

**Phase 4 (36+ months) — Future Actions**
13. Green hydrogen demand integration (tracks state-level progress)
14. Carbon market participation (tracks regulatory development)
15. Residential distributed generation at scale (post-market opening Dec 2027)

## 6. Critical Considerations and Risks

### 6.1 Post-Flood Recovery Context

The May 2024 floods are both an opportunity and a constraint. **Opportunity**: unlocks emergency and recovery financing (DFI post-disaster windows, climate adaptation funds); creates political will for resilience investments; makes the case for community solar+storage self-evident. **Constraint**: municipal fiscal stress limits co-financing capacity; institutional bandwidth is consumed by reconstruction; damaged infrastructure may need replacement before upgrades.

**Recommendation**: Frame grid resilience and community solar as reconstruction investments, not new initiatives. Embed energy resilience into the R$ 6 billion drainage and flood protection program rather than competing for separate funding.

### 6.2 Utility Privatization and Grid Governance

The privatization of CEEE (now operated by CPFL Energia / Equatorial) has led to reported deterioration in service quality — longer outages, slower repairs, reduced maintenance crews. This is the single biggest risk for grid resilience actions, because **the municipality does not control the distribution network**. Grid upgrades require utility cooperation or regulatory mandates from ANEEL.

**Recommendation**: Structure grid resilience as a co-investment with the concessionaire, leveraging regulatory pressure (ANEEL quality standards) and DFI financing to align incentives. Micro-grid and community solar+storage partially bypass this dependency.

### 6.3 Financing Constraints

Municipal budget is constrained post-flood. Actions requiring large upfront municipal investment score lower on feasibility. The portfolio should emphasize:
- **Quick-payback actions** (efficiency, LED) that generate savings to fund subsequent investments
- **PPP/ESCO models** (solar, LED) that shift capital expenditure to private partners
- **External financing** (DFI, climate funds) for larger actions (grid resilience, e-buses, community solar)
- **Revenue-generating actions** (biomethane offtake, carbon credits) that create ongoing income streams

### 6.4 Regulatory Evolution (Net Metering, Free Market Opening)

Brazil's energy market is in transition. The current MMGD net billing framework enables distributed generation but is under review. The free market opening (commercial Aug 2026, residential Dec 2027) will create new business models but also uncertainty.

**Impact on actions**:
- **Solar PV on public buildings**: Low risk — self-consumption model works regardless of net metering changes
- **Community solar**: Medium risk — shared generation structure depends on regulatory stability; monitor ANEEL decisions
- **Residential distributed generation**: Higher risk — business model depends on rules not yet finalized; classify as Phase 4

**Recommendation**: Prioritize self-consumption models (public buildings) over models dependent on credits or retail supply. Structure community solar under the shared-generation regulatory framework (consortium/cooperative) which has the strongest legal basis.

### 6.5 Just Transition Imperatives

RS's two coal plants (695 MW combined) will eventually close under the TEJ plan. While this is primarily a state/regional issue (Baixo Jacuí and Campanha regions), POA's energy actions should demonstrate that the clean energy transition creates local jobs and economic opportunity.

**Relevant considerations**:
- Solar PV installation and maintenance creates local technical jobs
- Biomethane and biogas operations require skilled workers
- E-bus maintenance is a new skill set that can be developed locally
- Community solar governance creates local institutional capacity

The portfolio should include workforce development and local supply chain requirements in procurement criteria — this strengthens financing applications (funders prioritize just transition narratives) and builds political support.
